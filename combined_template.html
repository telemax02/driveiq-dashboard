How DriveIQ works

DriveIQ turns each vehicle's own GPS tracking data into a simple, fair safety score. Every score is built from how the vehicle was actually driven — not from anyone's opinion — and the exact same rules are applied to every vehicle. Here's how it works, in plain English.

Fair by design: same rules for everyone, based on real data, using thresholds that match recognised industry standards.
What is DriveIQ?
Every vehicle has a GPS tracker that records how it moves — its speed, location and direction. DriveIQ reads that data and gives each journey a safety score out of 100. Because it's built entirely from the vehicle's own data, every driver is measured the same way, on facts rather than judgement.
What does it look at?
The four behaviours that matter most for safety:
• Speeding — driving over the road's speed limit.
• Harsh braking — slowing down much harder than normal.
• Harsh acceleration — speeding up much faster than normal.
• Harsh cornering — taking a corner too fast or too sharply.
Smooth, everyday driving isn't flagged — only the genuinely sharp or unsafe moments count.
What counts as a "trip"?
A trip is a single journey — from when the engine starts to when it's switched off. Parking ends the trip. So a morning drive and an evening drive are scored separately, and an overnight park is never lumped together into one long trip.
How is speeding decided?
DriveIQ knows the posted speed limit for the road from mapping data. You're only flagged when you go clearly over that limit — there's a small allowance built in, so a momentary GPS wobble or being a fraction over doesn't count against you. A confirmed speeding incident (the serious kind) only counts when the vehicle is at least 20 km/h over the limit for at least 30 seconds.
How are harsh braking, acceleration and cornering decided?
These come down to how forcefully the vehicle changes speed or direction — the kind of push you feel in your seat when someone brakes or corners hard. Gentle driving stays well under the line; only firm, jolting movements register. Each one is graded Mild, Moderate or Severe so you can see how strong it was.
Is it fair? Does it use industry standards?
Yes — and deliberately so. The points at which a movement counts as "harsh", and the small speeding buffer, are set to match the levels used right across the fleet-safety and vehicle-insurance industry. We haven't invented our own stricter rules — the thresholds line up with recognised industry benchmarks, so a score here means the same thing it would on other professional systems.
How is a trip scored?
Each trip is judged on those four behaviours and given a score out of 100 — higher is better. Speeding carries the most weight because it's the biggest safety risk, followed by braking, cornering and acceleration. The star rating is just a quick visual of the same score (five stars = as close to perfect as it gets).
How is a vehicle's overall score worked out?
It's the average of all that vehicle's trips, weighted by distance — so a long highway trip counts for more than a quick run down the road, which reflects real driving. Scores are shown to one decimal place so two vehicles that are very close can still be told apart.
Are short trips treated fairly?
Absolutely. A short trip driven perfectly scores just as highly as a long one — you're never marked down simply for a trip being short. Tiny movements, like shuffling around a car park, are left out entirely so they can't distort anything.
What do the maps show?
The map on each trip shows the real route the vehicle drove. If anything was flagged — speeding, harsh braking, cornering or acceleration — there's a marker at the exact spot it happened. Click a trip's map to open it full-size, then tap any marker to see what happened and when.
What if the speed limit for a road isn't known?
DriveIQ never guesses. If reliable speed-limit data isn't available for part of a trip, speeding simply isn't scored there — so you're never penalised on an assumption.
Why are two drivers on the same score ranked differently?
Behind the number on screen there's a more precise score. Two drivers might both show 99 — but one may be 99.4 and the other 99.1. The ranking always reflects that real difference, so the order is never down to chance.

Every vehicle is measured by exactly the same rules, on its own real driving data — and that's what makes the comparison fair.
